üîç Critical Population Size Effects

Both models fail completely at N=50, suggesting a critical population threshold below which consensus mechanisms break down.

Above N=75, external zealots achieve perfect consensus while internal zealots show rapid improvement from 73% to 99%+ as group size increases.

This suggests that larger populations provide more "opinion space" for consensus formation, particularly benefiting internal zealot dynamics.

üîÆ Theoretical Implications & Real-World Applications

Critical Population Dynamics

The universal failure at N=50 reveals that consensus mechanisms require a minimum "critical mass" - below this threshold, neither external influence nor internal commitment can overcome stochastic fluctuations.

External Zealots (Influencer Model)

External zealots represent media, institutions, or leaders that influence without competing for population space. Key insights:

  • Predictable degradation: Performance declines gradually as zealot count increases
  • Scale robust: Maintains high performance across population sizes N‚â•75
  • Applications: Mass media campaigns, institutional influence, external advocacy

Internal Zealots (Committed Minority Model)

Internal zealots compete within the population, creating complex dynamics. Key insights:

  • Sharp thresholds: Consensus exhibits cliff-like drops at critical zealot densities
  • Scale advantages: Larger populations provide more "opinion space" for consensus formation
  • Applications: Grassroots movements, organizational change, political activism

Strategic Implications

These findings suggest different optimal strategies:

  • For external influence: Focus on message reach and consistency
  • For internal movements: Carefully manage zealot density and target larger populations
  • For system designers: Ensure populations exceed critical thresholds before deploying consensus mechanisms
